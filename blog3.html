Should Colleges Require Students to get Internships as a Graduation requirement?

Published on November 16, 2025

Colleges should require students to complete an internship before graduation because internships give students real-world experience, help them build professionalism, and allow them to discover what they actually want to do. My internship at the Morgridge Institute for Research showed me firsthand how much more prepared, capable, and motivated a student becomes once they’ve stepped outside the classroom and into real work, especially IT focused careers. If colleges truly want students to graduate ready for their careers, internships should be essential.


Students should be required to complete internships because they are the most effective bridge between academic learning and real-world careers. No matter how strong an educational program is, lectures and homework can only take students so far. An internship forces students to apply what they’ve learned in an environment where the consequences are real and the standards are higher. It’s this jump from controlled classroom exercises to authentic work that gives students the preparation they need. Think high school to college, it's just another level up in your education and career journey!


The first reason colleges should require internships is that they provide hands-on skills that the classroom simply can’t replicate. During my internship, I got to learn about the technology I was using, while I was using it. I got to work with different JavaScript frameworks like Liquid and NodeJS to update various websites. These tools weren’t hypothetical or used for class projects, I was working on websites that actual people navigate through daily, nationwide. Learning how to navigate these new coding languages, troubleshoot issues, and follow actual research workflows gave me technical knowledge I could never have gained in a traditional class. Internships let students move beyond theory into application, and that experience makes them far more capable when they graduate.


Second, internships help students develop professional confidence and maturity. Many graduates struggle with the transition from college assignments to workplace expectations. During my internship, I learned how to work with supervisors, communicate updates clearly, ask better questions, and adjust my work when things didn’t go as planned. I learned what it feels like to be part of a team and how to stay organized in a fast-moving environment. These skills are foundational to success in any career. They are also very difficult to teach in a classroom setting. An internship forces students to practice these habits in a real setting, and that experience builds confidence that carries into every future job.


Finally, internships help students figure out what they actually want from their careers. Many students graduate unsure of their direction simply because they’ve never tried working in a real environment. My internship helped me realize that I enjoy technical, research-focused work and want to continue building those skills, while creating artful websites. Other students might discover they want something completely different, only because they got to experience their internship. Requiring internships ensures that every student has the chance to explore, test their interests, and make informed decisions before graduating. In an increasingly expensive world, this is essential for preparing for life ahead of college.


Colleges should require internships because they prepare students for the world they’re about to enter. My experience at the Morgridge Institute for Research showed me how transformative an internship can be, both personally and professionally. If colleges truly want to equip students for success, making internships mandatory is the smartest step forward.
